# Hanging Wallpaper

Modern paste-the-wall wallpapers make hanging significantly easier than the traditional paste-the-paper method, but the fundamentals are the same: perfect prep, a plumb first drop, and patience with the seams.

## Wall preparation

Wallpaper reveals every flaw in the wall behind it. Fill nail holes and cracks with filler and sand flush. Sand old paint gloss to a matte finish with 180-grit paper. Wash walls with sugar soap solution and let dry overnight.

Apply a coat of wallpaper primer or diluted PVA. This gives the paste something to grip and — crucially — makes the paper strippable in future without ripping chunks of plaster off the wall.

## Setting out

Never start at a corner. Corners are almost never plumb and starting there will send the pattern off-vertical for the whole room.

Measure the width of your paper and mark a point that width, minus 10mm, from your chosen starting corner. Drop a plumb line at this mark and pencil a vertical guide. Your first drop will follow this line — the 10mm turns into the corner as an overlap.

## Cutting drops

Measure the wall height and add 100mm top and bottom for trimming. Roll out the paper on a pasting table with the pattern face-up and cut with a long steel rule and a sharp knife.

For a pattern with a repeat, match subsequent drops to the same point on the repeat before cutting. Number the back of each drop in sequence so you don't get confused halfway up a wall.

## Pasting

Follow the paper's instructions — paste the wall for paste-the-wall papers, paste the paper for traditional. Use a wallpaper paste rated for your paper weight; heavy vinyls need thicker paste than lightweight non-woven.

Paste evenly, especially at the edges. Dry edges are the number-one cause of lifting seams weeks later.

## Hanging

Hang the first drop against your plumb line. Let 50mm run onto the ceiling and 50mm onto the skirting for trimming. Smooth from the top down and centre-out with a wallpaper brush — never a cloth, which drags the paste — pushing bubbles toward the edges.

Butt subsequent drops edge-to-edge; do not overlap. A slight overlap will show as a shadow line under sidelight. Roll seams gently with a seam roller after 10 minutes to bed them down.

## Trimming

Trim the top and bottom with a broad putty knife held tight against the ceiling or skirting, cutting along the knife with a fresh blade. Change the blade every few cuts — a dull knife tears wet paper.

Wipe stray paste off woodwork immediately with a damp sponge. Dried paste on a painted skirting takes serious effort to remove.
