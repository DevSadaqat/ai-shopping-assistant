# Kitchen Splashback Tiling

A tiled splashback is one of the highest-impact upgrades in a kitchen and is genuinely a weekend project. The trick is precise cutting around outlets and edges.

## Surface prep

Remove old paint that's flaking or shiny with a scraper and sand the wall with 120-grit paper to give a mechanical key. Fill any holes with lightweight filler and sand flat.

Wipe the whole surface with sugar soap and a damp cloth to lift kitchen grease — even a thin residue prevents adhesive from bonding properly. Let dry completely.

Isolate power to the kitchen at the switchboard before removing any outlet cover plates. You can pull cover plates and work around outlets yourself, but leave the wiring behind them alone.

## Layout

Measure the height and width you want to cover. Standard splashback runs from the bench surface up to the underside of the wall cabinets. Find the visual centre — usually behind the cooktop — and dry-lay a row of tiles across a straight edge on the floor to work out where cuts will fall.

If a full tile lines up too close to an outlet, shift the whole layout by half a tile so your cuts are cleaner. A cut fragment less than a third of the tile width looks wrong; plan around it.

## Adhesive and setting

Use a wall-tile adhesive rated for kitchens (heat and moisture resistant). Trowel a thin, even bed onto no more than a square metre at a time — any more and the adhesive will skin before you tile into it.

Comb ridges with a 6mm notched trowel. Set each tile with a slight twist and use 2mm spacers at every corner. Wipe stray adhesive off the tile face immediately — dried thinset is a job to remove.

## Around outlets

Measure the outlet cutout carefully and score the cut line with a tile scorer or a small angle grinder fitted with a diamond blade. A grinder gives you internal cuts you can't get from a manual scorer.

Wear safety glasses and a dust mask when grinding tile. Silica dust is not something to breathe.

## Grouting

Wait 24 hours after tiling. Use an epoxy or urethane grout for kitchens — cement grout stains from cooking oils and coffee within a year. Work the grout diagonally with a rubber float, then wipe with a damp sponge before it hazes.

Silicone the joint where the splashback meets the benchtop, not grout. That joint moves with thermal expansion and cement grout will crack there within months.
