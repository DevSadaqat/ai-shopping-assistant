# Interior Painting

Most bad paint jobs fail at prep, not at the final coat. Spend twice as long preparing surfaces as you spend rolling and the finish will look sprayed on.

## Surface preparation

Wash walls with sugar soap solution to remove skin oils, cooking residue, and old dust. Rinse with clean water and let dry overnight. Fill holes with lightweight filler — small holes get one pass, larger holes need two coats sanded flat between passes. Sand all filled areas and any glossy patches with 180-grit paper so primer has a mechanical key.

Mask trims, skirting, and window frames with painter's tape. Press the tape edge down firmly with a putty knife to prevent bleed.

## Priming

Use a primer suited to the substrate: acrylic primer-sealer over bare plaster, stain-blocking primer over water stains or nicotine, oil-based primer over glossy or previously enamelled surfaces. Never skip primer on bare plaster — the wall will suck moisture out of your topcoat and produce a patchy, flashy finish.

Roll primer with a 10mm nap roller for smooth walls, 12mm for lightly textured surfaces. One even coat is usually sufficient.

## Cutting in

Cutting in is painting the edges where a roller can't reach — corners, ceiling lines, around trims. Use a 50mm angled sash brush loaded no more than one-third of its length. Draw a clean line about 25mm out from the edge, then move the brush along the edge with a light hand to place the sharp line exactly where you want it.

Cut in the top and one wall at a time, then roll immediately while the cut-in is wet. Otherwise the join will show as a lap line.

## Rolling technique

Load the roller from the tray until it stops absorbing paint but is not dripping. Roll onto the wall in an M or W pattern to distribute paint, then fill in the gaps with straight vertical passes. Finish each section with one long, light stroke top to bottom — this lays off any roller texture. Work in three-foot sections and keep a wet edge; if the previous section starts to dry before you get to the next, you'll see lap marks.

## Second coat

Wait the full recoat time listed on the can — usually 2 to 4 hours. Rolling a second coat before the first has properly set drags the underlying paint and produces a mottled finish.
