# Garden Irrigation Setup

A drip irrigation system on a timer will save you hundreds of hours over a summer and use a fraction of the water sprinklers do. It's a straightforward DIY install once you understand the components.

## Plan the layout

Sketch your garden beds and vegetable rows to scale. Note the tap you'll draw from and the distance to the furthest plant. For most home gardens, one 13mm main polyline can serve up to 30 to 40 metres of run before pressure drops noticeably.

Group plants by water demand — vegetables need daily watering in summer, established shrubs need weekly. Different zones ideally get separate valves so you can water each on its own schedule.

## Components

You need:

- A tap connection with backflow prevention (mandatory in most jurisdictions).
- A pressure regulator — most drip components are rated at 100 to 200 kPa; mains pressure is 400+ kPa and will blow fittings apart.
- A filter — cheap, essential. Grit from the mains supply blocks emitters otherwise.
- 13mm poly main line.
- 4mm drip line or individual emitters.
- Barbed T-fittings, elbows, and end plugs.
- A battery-powered timer, or a solenoid valve wired to a controller if you want scheduling.

## Installation

Run the 13mm main line above ground along the back edge of each garden bed. Bury it 100mm under paths where it crosses, and use a plastic sleeve for protection where it crosses driveways.

Punch holes in the main line at each plant location with a proper hole punch (do not use a nail — the hole won't seal around the T-fitting). Push a barbed T into each hole and connect a 4mm drip line to the plant.

Use one emitter per small plant, two or three for larger shrubs, and space in-line emitters at 300mm along vegetable rows.

## Test and adjust

Turn the water on for two minutes and walk the whole system. Look for spray, drips at the fittings, and dry spots at the emitters. Punch new emitters or add T-fittings as needed. A well-tuned system waters every plant evenly without pooling anywhere.

## Timer scheduling

Water in the early morning — evaporation is minimal and disease pressure on foliage is lower than watering at night. For established gardens, a longer, less frequent cycle drives roots deeper than short, daily bursts. Twice a week for 30 minutes usually beats every day for 8 minutes.

Check the system fortnightly through summer for blocked emitters and disconnected drippers.
