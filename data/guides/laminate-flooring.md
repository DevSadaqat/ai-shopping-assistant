# Laminate Flooring Installation

Laminate is one of the easier finishes for a DIYer. The install is genuinely fast once the subfloor is right, but skimping on prep produces a floor that clicks apart within a year.

## Subfloor prep

The subfloor must be flat within 3mm over any 2m span. Check with a straightedge. Grind or self-level high and low spots — laminate has no give and any dip becomes a hinge point that stresses the joints.

Concrete subfloors need a moisture check. Tape a 300mm-square piece of clear plastic sheet to the slab overnight; if you see condensation on the underside in the morning, the slab is too wet and needs sealing or a proper moisture barrier before you lay anything.

## Underlay

Roll a laminate underlay across the whole floor, butting seams (not overlapping) and taping the joins. Choose an underlay rated for your substrate — some brands include a built-in vapour barrier for use over concrete.

## Acclimatisation

Store the boxes flat in the room where they'll be laid, at least 48 hours before installation. Laminate expands and contracts with humidity; if you install cold boards in a warm room, they'll grow and the joints will pop.

## Expansion gap

This is the single most important detail. Laminate is a floating floor — it isn't fixed to anything, and it needs room to move as humidity changes across seasons. Leave a 10mm gap between the boards and every wall, every doorway trim, and every fixed obstacle. Skirting or scotia covers the gap afterwards.

Skip the expansion gap and the floor will buckle in the first humid summer.

## Laying

Start along the longest wall with the tongue side facing in. Cut the first board so that the last board in the row ends up at least 300mm long — a short piece at the end of a row looks amateurish and is structurally weak.

Stagger board joints from row to row by at least 300mm. Click each board into the previous one at a shallow angle, then press flat. A tapping block and a rubber mallet close joints tightly without damaging the tongue.

Slice under door jambs with a jamb saw so a board can slide underneath — this looks far better than cutting the board around the jamb.

## Trims and beading

Fix skirting or quarter-round to the wall, not to the floor. If a fixing goes through the laminate into the subfloor, you've pinned the floor and defeated the point of a floating install.
