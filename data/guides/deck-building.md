# Deck Building: Frame, Boards, and Finish

A backyard deck is a substantial project but well within DIY scope if you plan the frame properly. Get the frame right and the deck boards are the easy part.

## Planning and permits

Check local council rules before you start. Decks over a certain height (typically 1m in most jurisdictions) require a permit and engineered plans. Even below that threshold, structural loading rules apply.

## Bearers and joists

Bearers sit on posts and run in the primary span direction. Joists sit on the bearers, perpendicular, at 450mm centres for standard decking timber. Use H3-treated pine or hardwood — anything else in ground or splash-prone conditions will rot within five years.

Check level across the whole frame with a long straightedge and a spirit level. Any twist or dip in the frame will telegraph up through the deck boards. Slope the frame away from the house by 5mm per metre so water sheds.

## Fixing the frame

Bearer-to-post connections should be with galvanised M12 coach screws or engineered post-anchors — not nails. Joist-to-bearer connections use joist hangers or triple-grip brackets, again galvanised. Every fixing must be corrosion-rated for the exposure class of your site.

## Deck boards

Lay deck boards with a consistent 4mm to 6mm gap between them for drainage and thermal movement. A framing nail laid flat between boards makes a good spacer while you fix.

Hidden fasteners give the cleanest look and eliminate the raised nail heads that trip bare feet, but they slow the job. Traditional screw-fixing through the face of the board is faster; use decking screws (not standard timber screws) and pre-drill hardwoods to avoid splitting.

Stagger board joints across joists — don't line joints up in a row.

## Finish

Let hardwood boards weather for six to eight weeks before applying oil. Fresh timber contains mill glaze that stops oil penetrating. Apply decking oil with a wide brush along the grain, working in from the far side of the deck toward your exit. Two thin coats penetrate better than one thick one.

Reoil every 12 to 18 months. Don't wait until the boards look grey — by then, UV has already broken down the surface fibres and the oil job is really a sanding job.
